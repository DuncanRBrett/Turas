# Your Brand Health Report — User Guide

**For:** Brand managers and marketing teams\
**What this is:** A guide to reading and using your Turas Brand Health
Report without needing the research team in the room.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## What this report is

Your Brand Health Report measures how your brand is performing across
every stage of the buyer's journey — from whether people have heard of
you, to whether they think of you in the right situations, to whether
they're buying you.

The report is a self-contained file that opens in any web browser. No
login, no internet connection, no special software required. You can
share the file directly, open it on a laptop in a meeting room, or save
it to OneDrive.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Opening the report

Double-click the `.html` file. It opens in your default browser (Chrome,
Edge, Safari — all work). If it doesn't open, right-click and choose
"Open with" → your browser.

Everything is in one file. You don't need any other files.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Getting your bearings — the main navigation

At the top of every page you'll see a row of tabs:

```         
Summary  |  [Category 1]  |  [Category 2]  |  Portfolio  |  Pinned Views  |  About
```

-   **Summary** — your one-page executive view across all categories
-   **[Category name] tabs** — one tab per category in the study (e.g.
    Dry Seasonings & Spices, Ready Meals). All the detail lives here.
-   **Portfolio** — how your brand sits across all categories. Only
    shown when multiple categories are included.
-   **Pinned Views** — charts and tables you've saved for easy access
    (your personal selection)
-   **About** — methodology notes for when someone asks "how was this
    measured?"

Click any tab to jump to it. The report remembers nothing between
sessions — pinned views are not saved when you close the browser.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Summary tab

The Summary tab gives you the headline picture in one view. Use it for
exec check-ins and quick team catch-ups.

**What you'll see:**

**Category headline cards** — one card per category. Each card shows
your brand's key metrics at a glance: - *Awareness* — what percentage of
category buyers have heard of your brand - *Consideration* — what
percentage hold a positive attitude (love + prefer) - *Penetration* —
what percentage bought your brand in the target period - *Mental Market
Share (MMS)* — the share of category entry points your brand "wins" in
buyers' minds

These cards are your first checkpoint. A high awareness but low MMS
means people know you exist but don't think of you when they're buying.
A high MMS but low penetration means mental presence isn't converting —
worth digging into the funnel.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Category tabs

Each category tab has its own set of subtabs. Click the subtab row (just
below the main tab) to switch between:

```         
Brand Funnel  |  Mental Availability  |  Word of Mouth  |  Category Buying
```

### Subtab 1: Brand Funnel

The funnel shows the conversion rate from awareness to loyalty, one
brand at a time. Your brand is highlighted in your primary colour;
competitors are shown in grey.

**The stages:**

| Stage | What it measures |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Awareness | "Have you heard of this brand?" — the widest point of the funnel |
| Disposition | Positive attitude — "love" + "prefer" combined. These are people who would willingly choose you. |
| Penetration | Bought in the target period (e.g. last 3 months). Actual recent behaviour. |
| Primary | Bought most often. Your most loyal buyers. |

**How to read it:** Look at where the funnel narrows most sharply. If
you drop steeply from Awareness to Disposition, people know you but
don't like you enough — a brand image issue. If you drop steeply from
Disposition to Penetration, people like you but aren't buying — a
distribution or price issue.

**The chart controls:**

-   *Brand selector* — click any brand chip at the top to show or hide
    brands
-   *Slope / Bar toggle* — switch between a waterfall slope view and a
    bar chart view. The slope view makes conversion losses visible; the
    bar view makes comparisons between brands clearer.
-   *Attitude breakdown* — a panel below the funnel shows how the full
    attitude spectrum breaks down: love, prefer, ambivalent, reject, no
    opinion. Your brand's rejection rate compared to competitors can
    surface image problems before they reach the funnel.

**Insight box** — below the chart, a text insight box summarises the
most important finding from this category automatically.

### Subtab 2: Mental Availability

Mental Availability measures how strongly your brand is linked to the
buying situations (Category Entry Points, or CEPs) that drive purchase
in this category.

This is grounded in the Ehrenberg-Bass / Romaniuk framework: buyers
don't deliberate — they retrieve. Whichever brand comes to mind first in
a relevant situation gets bought most. This tab shows whether your brand
is winning those mental moments.

**Key metrics:**

| Metric | What it means |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| **MMS** (Mental Market Share) | The share of all (respondent × CEP) mental associations your brand wins. The single best summary of your mental position. |
| **MPen** (Mental Penetration) | The percentage of category buyers who link your brand to at least one CEP. Measures reach — how widely known your brand is in context. |
| **NS** (Network Size) | The average number of CEPs each of your buyers links you to. Measures depth — how richly connected your brand is. |

A strong brand has high MMS driven by both breadth (high MPen) and depth
(high NS). If your MPen is high but NS is low, many people know you for
only one thing — you're fragile. If your NS is high but MPen is low,
your loyal buyers love you but you're not reaching enough people.

**The CEP × Brand matrix:**

The main chart shows a matrix: CEPs on the left, brands across the top,
with the strength of each brand–CEP association shown as a percentage.
Your brand's column is highlighted.

-   Dark cells = strong association
-   Light cells = weak association

Look for: 1. **CEPs your brand doesn't own that your main competitor
does** — these are gaps to close 2. **CEPs where every brand scores
low** — opportunities to own neglected territory 3. **CEPs your brand
uniquely leads** — distinctive positions worth protecting

**The brand attribute panel:**

Below or alongside the CEP matrix, you'll see brand image attributes —
perception items like "good value" or "high quality ingredients." These
show how strongly each brand owns functional and emotional perceptions.
Attributes feed the Drivers & Barriers analysis.

**TURF optimisation:**

If this is available in your report, the TURF (Total Unduplicated Reach
and Frequency) panel answers: "If we could only focus on N CEPs, which
combination would reach the most buyers mentally?" Use this for media
planning and creative strategy — it tells you which situations to own
first.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Subtab 3: Word of Mouth

WOM measures word-of-mouth activity in the category — how much people
are hearing and sharing positive and negative conversation about each
brand.

**The four measures:**

| Measure | What it shows |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Received positive | \% who have heard someone recommend this brand |
| Received negative | \% who have heard someone criticise this brand |
| Shared positive | \% who have personally recommended this brand |
| Shared negative | \% who have personally said something negative about this brand |

**How to read it:** Positive WOM is an amplifier — it helps awareness
and disposition grow without paid media spend. Negative WOM is a risk.
The chart shows your brand against competitors so you can see whether
you're above or below average on each measure.

**Net WOM:** The key derived metric is the balance between positive and
negative. A brand generating far more positive than negative WOM is in a
healthy word-of-mouth position. A brand where negative is catching up
with positive should investigate rejection themes in the Funnel tab.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Subtab 4: Category Buying

Category Buying shows how the category itself is being bought — how
often, by whom, and across how many brands.

**What you'll see:**

**Category buying frequency** — how often category buyers are purchasing
in this category (daily, weekly, monthly, occasionally). This sets the
context for your penetration numbers.

**Repertoire analysis** — how many brands each buyer is purchasing. Most
FMCG categories show buyers purchasing 2–4 brands. This tells you how
"open" the category is to multi-brand buying versus brand loyalty.

**Share of Requirements (SoR)** — of all the purchases a buyer makes in
this category, what share goes to your brand? High SoR means loyal
buyers; low SoR means you're one of many in the basket.

**Buyer heaviness** — the split of your buyer base between light,
medium, and heavy buyers. Ehrenberg-Bass research consistently shows
that growth comes from reaching light and occasional buyers, not from
deepening loyalty with heavy buyers. This chart shows whether your buyer
profile matches the theoretical norm for a brand of your size.

## Portfolio tab (multi-category studies)

The Portfolio tab is only shown when the study covers two or more
categories. It answers: how is your brand positioned across the full
portfolio of categories you compete in?

### Overview subtab

The Overview shows your brand's cross-category footprint in one view.

**Focal brand picker:** At the top of this subtab is a dropdown to
switch the focal brand. If you want to see the portfolio picture from a
competitor's perspective — how they look across all the same categories
— select them here.

**Hero KPIs (top row of cards):**

-   *Categories with presence* — how many categories your brand is
    aware-of in
-   *Average awareness* — your average awareness across those categories
-   *Deep-dive categories* — how many categories in this study have full
    CBM analysis
-   *Total categories tracked* — the total number of categories in the
    study

**Ranked bar chart:** All categories ranked by your brand's awareness in
each one. Taller bars = stronger presence. Deep-dive categories are
tagged with a badge.

**Category detail table:** One row per category. Columns: - *Category* —
name - *Type* — Deep-dive or Awareness-only - *Cat. usage* — what
proportion of respondents buy in this category at all - *Focal
awareness* — your brand's awareness among that category's buyers -
*Rank* — your brand's rank on awareness within the category (#1 of 5,
etc.) - *Gap to leader* — how many percentage points behind the
category's most aware brand you are (shows "Leader" if that's you) -
*Penetration, SCR, Vol share, Freq* — buying depth metrics, available
for deep-dive categories only

**Deep-dive competitive context cards:** For each full-analysis
category, a card showing your brand's SCR (Share of Category
Requirements) and penetration, alongside a ranked list of the top 5
brands.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Footprint subtab

The Footprint shows a heatmap — categories on one axis, brands on the
other — with awareness as the cell colour intensity. Use this to spot: -
Where your brand has presence vs gaps - Where competitors are
strongest - Whether your brand's footprint overlaps heavily with key
competitors (suggesting direct competition) or is distinct

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Competitive Set subtab

Shows which categories your main competitors also appear in. Useful for
identifying where you're fighting the same brands across multiple
categories vs where you have a cleaner run.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Category Context subtab

Shows how each category is performing on overall market size and
dynamics — helping you prioritise which categories are most important to
your portfolio.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Extension subtab

Shows how your brand's awareness in one (home) category translates to a
potential foundation in other categories. If buyers of your home
category are aware of you in adjacent categories, extension into those
categories starts from a stronger position.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Pinned Views

The pin icon (📌) appears in the toolbar of most chart and table panels.
Clicking it opens a small menu:

-   **Chart** — saves the current chart
-   **Table** — saves the data table
-   **Both** — saves both

Pinned items appear in the **Pinned Views** tab, collected in one place.
Use this during a meeting to build a running summary of the most
important findings as you go through the report. Pinned Views can be
exported to PNG or Excel from within the tab.

**Important:** Pinned views are not saved permanently. If you close the
browser and re-open the file, the pins are gone. To share pinned
content, use the export buttons before closing.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Exporting charts and data

**PNG export (chart image):** In any panel toolbar, click the camera
icon. A high-resolution PNG of the current chart is downloaded to your
browser's default download folder. Use this to paste charts into
PowerPoint or Word.

**Excel export (data table):** In any panel toolbar, click the Excel
icon. Downloads the underlying data table as an `.xlsx` file.

**From Pinned Views:** The Pinned Views tab has a bulk export option at
the top — export all pinned items at once to a single PNG slideshow or
Excel workbook.

## Tips for getting the most from the report in a meeting

1.  **Start with Summary** — set the scene before diving into detail.
    The headline cards tell the story in 30 seconds.

2.  **Navigate by question, not by tab** — think about what you want to
    know first, then go to the right tab. "Why aren't we converting
    awareness?" → Brand Funnel. "Which buying situations should we
    target?" → Mental Availability TURF.

3.  **Use the focal brand picker on Portfolio** — switching to a
    competitor in Portfolio Overview is often the moment that sparks the
    most useful discussion in a meeting.

4.  **Pin as you go** — when you find an important finding, pin it
    immediately. By the end of the session you have a curated shortlist
    ready to export.

5.  **The attitude breakdown matters** — the rejection rate in the
    Funnel tab is often the finding that surprises clients most. If your
    brand has a higher rejection rate than competitors, that's an image
    issue that won't fix itself through more advertising.

6.  **Don't average the funnel** — a 50% awareness brand and an 80%
    awareness brand have very different conversion dynamics. Always read
    the funnel in the context of your brand's total size.

## Glossary of key terms

| Term | Plain-English meaning |
|------------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| **Awareness** | The percentage of category buyers who say they've heard of a brand. The widest part of the funnel. |
| **Disposition / Consideration** | The percentage who say they would positively choose a brand (love + prefer combined). Sometimes called favourable attitude. |
| **Penetration** | The percentage who have bought the brand in the measured period (e.g. last 3 months). Actual behaviour, not intention. |
| **Primary brand** | The brand someone buys most often in a category. Reflects loyal buyers specifically. |
| **MMS (Mental Market Share)** | The share of mental category associations your brand wins across all buyers and all CEPs. The single best summary of mental strength. |
| **MPen (Mental Penetration)** | The percentage of buyers who link your brand to at least one CEP. Breadth of mental reach. |
| **NS (Network Size)** | The average number of CEPs buyers associate with your brand. Depth of mental reach. |
| **CEP (Category Entry Point)** | A buying situation or use occasion — e.g. "when I want something the whole family will enjoy." CEPs are how buyers think about categories before they think about brands. |
| **WOM (Word of Mouth)** | Conversations about brands — both receiving and sharing, positive and negative. |
| **SoR (Share of Requirements)** | Of all purchases a buyer makes in the category, the proportion that go to a specific brand. 30% SoR means 3 in every 10 of their category purchases are your brand. |
| **TURF** | Total Unduplicated Reach and Frequency — an optimisation that finds the combination of CEPs that reaches the most buyers mentally. Used for media and creative strategy. |
| **Repertoire** | The set of brands a buyer purchases across in a category. Most FMCG buyers have a repertoire of 2–5 brands. |
| **DBA (Distinctive Brand Assets)** | Logos, colours, taglines, characters — any visual or audio element people associate with your brand. The DBA section shows how famous (widely recognised) and unique (attributed to your brand, not a competitor) each asset is. |
| **Deep-dive category** | A category that has the full CBM battery — awareness, CEPs, attitude, penetration, WOM. Contrasted with awareness-only categories where only cross-category awareness was collected. |
| **Focal brand** | The client's own brand — the one whose performance this report is primarily tracking. |

## About the methodology

The **About** tab (last tab in the navigation) contains the full
methodology note — question wording, sample sizes, significance testing
approach, and academic references. Share this with anyone who asks "how
was this measured?"

Key points: - Significance testing uses two-proportion z-tests with
Bonferroni correction across brands - Sample sizes and base sizes are
shown in the data tables within each panel - The framework is grounded
in Romaniuk & Sharp's *Better Brand Health* (2022) and Byron Sharp's
*How Brands Grow* (2010)
